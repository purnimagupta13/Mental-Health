Social Anxiety Disorder

Social anxiety disorder, also called social phobia, is a mental health condition where everyday interactions cause significant anxiety, self-consciousness, and embarrassment due to fear of being judged negatively by others.

Fear and anxiety can lead to avoidance, disrupting relationships, daily routines, work, school, or other activities. With treatment, such as psychotherapy and medication, people can improve their confidence and ability to interact with others.

Symptoms

While shyness or discomfort in certain situations is normal, social anxiety disorder involves persistent fear, anxiety, and avoidance that interfere with daily life. Symptoms often begin in the early to mid-teens but can also start in childhood or adulthood.

Emotional and Behavioral Symptoms

  • Fear of situations where you may be judged negatively.
  • Worry about embarrassing or humiliating yourself.
  • Intense fear of interacting or talking with strangers.
  • Fear of physical symptoms like blushing, sweating, or trembling.
  • Avoiding activities or people out of fear of embarrassment.
  • Intense anxiety during social situations.
  • Overanalyzing performance and identifying flaws after interactions.

Physical Symptoms

  • Blushing.
  • Fast heartbeat.
  • Trembling or sweating.
  • Upset stomach or nausea.
  • Dizziness or lightheadedness.
  • Muscle tension.

Avoiding Common Social Situations

People with social anxiety disorder often avoid:

  • Interacting with unfamiliar people.
  • Attending social gatherings.
  • Starting conversations or making eye contact.
  • Eating in front of others.
  • Using public restrooms.

When to See a Doctor

If fear and avoidance of social situations cause significant distress or interfere with daily life, seek help from a healthcare or mental health professional.

Causes

Social anxiety disorder likely arises from a combination of biological and environmental factors, including:

  • Inherited traits and family history of anxiety disorders.
  • Overactive brain structure regulating fear (amygdala).
  • Learned behaviors from past negative social experiences.

Risk Factors

Factors that increase the risk of developing social anxiety disorder include:

  • Family history of anxiety disorders.
  • Negative experiences like bullying, rejection, or humiliation.
  • Shy or withdrawn temperament.
  • New social or work demands.
  • Appearance or conditions drawing attention, such as stuttering or facial disfigurement.

Complications

Untreated social anxiety disorder can lead to:

  • Low self-esteem and poor social skills.
  • Isolation and difficulty maintaining relationships.
  • Substance abuse.
  • Depression or suicidal thoughts.

Prevention

Although social anxiety disorder cannot always be prevented, steps to manage anxiety include:

  • Seeking help early.
  • Keeping a journal to track triggers and coping strategies.
  • Setting priorities to manage stress.
  • Avoiding unhealthy substance use.
