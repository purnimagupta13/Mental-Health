Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder

What is ADHD?

ADHD, or Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder, is a mental health condition that affects focus, impulse control, and activity levels. It can impact daily life, including work, school, and relationships.

Common Symptoms

People with ADHD may experience symptoms such as difficulty concentrating, forgetfulness, hyperactivity, and impulsiveness. Symptoms vary depending on the type of ADHD.

Types of ADHD

  • Predominantly Inattentive: Difficulty focusing, completing tasks, and following instructions.
  • Predominantly Hyperactive-Impulsive: Excessive movement, interrupting others, and difficulty waiting for turns.
  • Combined Type: Features both inattentive and hyperactive-impulsive symptoms.

Causes of ADHD

While the exact cause is unknown, factors involved in ADHD development may include:

  • Genetics: A family history of ADHD or other mental health disorders.
  • Environmental toxins, such as lead exposure.
  • Maternal drug, alcohol, or tobacco use during pregnancy.
  • Premature birth or low birth weight.

Diagnosis

ADHD diagnosis involves observing symptoms over time, gathering input from caregivers or teachers, and ruling out other conditions. Doctors may use checklists and rating scales for assessment.

Risk Factors

Several factors can increase the risk of ADHD, such as:

  • Having blood relatives with ADHD.
  • Exposure to environmental toxins.
  • Premature birth or low birth weight.

Treatment Options

  • Therapies: Behavioral therapy and psychotherapy can help manage symptoms and develop coping strategies.
  • Medications: Stimulants (e.g., Adderall, Ritalin) and non-stimulants (e.g., Strattera) are commonly prescribed.
  • Lifestyle Changes: Regular exercise, a healthy diet, adequate sleep, and structured routines can be beneficial.

ADHD in Adults

ADHD often persists into adulthood, causing challenges in time management, relationships, and work. Treatment and coping strategies are available to improve quality of life.

Prevention

While ADHD cannot always be prevented, these steps may help reduce risks:

  • Avoid alcohol, drugs, and smoking during pregnancy.
  • Protect children from exposure to toxins like lead paint.
  • Limit screen time, especially in the first five years of life.

Coexisting Conditions

ADHD may coexist with anxiety, depression, or learning disabilities, requiring a comprehensive treatment approach.
