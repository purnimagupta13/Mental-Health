Major Depressive Disorder

Depression, also referred to as clinical depression or major depressive disorder, is a medical condition that is classified as a mood disorder. It can affect how you feel and your ability to function on a day-to-day basis.

While everyone tends to feel sad or low from time to time, feeling that way for weeks or months at a time could mean you have depression.

If you suspect you may have depression, you’re not alone. Over 8% of adults living in the United States have experienced at least one major depressive episode.

Symptoms of Major Depressive Disorder

Everyone experiences depression differently. While some people may have a few symptoms, others may have many. The frequency, severity, and duration of the symptoms can also vary from person to person.

  • Feeling sad or low
  • Having an "empty" mood
  • Feeling anxious
  • Feeling guilty or helpless
  • Feeling worthless, hopeless, or pessimistic
  • Feeling restless, frustrated, or irritated
  • Losing interest in things you once enjoyed
  • Avoiding your usual activities
  • Having less energy and feeling fatigued
  • Moving or speaking slowly
  • Having difficulty paying attention, remembering, or making decisions
  • Having difficulty sleeping, waking up too early, or oversleeping
  • Experiencing unplanned changes in eating habits and weight
  • Experiencing headaches, cramps, digestive issues, or other aches and pains without a clear cause
  • Talking about death, having thoughts of suicide, or attempting self-harm

Causes of Major Depressive Disorder

The exact causes of major depressive disorder are not fully understood, but experts believe that several different factors contribute to its onset. These include:

  • Biochemistry: Differences in brain chemical levels can make you more prone to depression. While chemical imbalances are not the primary cause, neurotransmitters like serotonin and dopamine play important roles.
  • Genetics: Having a relative with depression increases your chances of developing it. However, the interaction of genetic and environmental factors determines the risk.
  • Personal Medical History: A history of depression increases the likelihood of future episodes.
  • Gender: Women are twice as likely to develop depression, partly due to hormonal and social factors.
  • Life Events: Trauma, loss, and major changes can trigger depression, especially if experienced in childhood.
  • Stress: Physical and mental stress can elevate the risk of depression.
  • Isolation: Lack of support and social withdrawal can contribute to depression.
  • Medical Conditions: Depression can accompany chronic conditions like heart disease, diabetes, and Parkinson’s disease.
  • Medication: Certain medications, such as anticonvulsants and corticosteroids, can have depression as a side effect.
  • Substances: Alcohol and drugs can cause or worsen depression, though they are sometimes used as coping mechanisms.
  • Personality: Traits like high neuroticism and low extraversion may increase vulnerability to depression.
